Hybrid Fast Crew Boat — 4-Source Power Architecture (Concept SLD)

42 m FCB3 × 100 kW gensets120 kVA battery converter75 kVA shoreLR class415/240 V 50 Hz

Split-bus main switchboard synchronising four sources: three diesel gensets, a battery/grid converter fed from a Li-ion bank (zero-emission "green cabin" mode at anchor/alongside), and an isolated shore connection. Distribution via motor control centres and power distribution boards; dedicated 24 V DC system with battery panels and gyro-stabiliser board.

How it works: Each genset feeds the 415 V bus through its own air circuit breaker; a synchroniser matches voltage, frequency and phase angle before any breaker closes onto a live bus, and load sharing is balanced through the power management system. The battery converter is a bidirectional inverter — at sea it rectifies surplus genset power to charge the Li-ion bank; in green-cabin mode it inverts DC back to 415 V and carries the hotel load alone so all engines stop. The bus-tie splits the board into two half-buses so a fault on one side cannot black out the other; essential consumers are duplicated port/starboard across the split. The shore incomer is mechanically and electrically interlocked so it can never parallel with the gensets — power is broken before transfer. Heavy consumers (75 kW bow thruster, 35 kW gyro stabiliser) connect directly to the MSB to keep voltage dip away from the distribution boards, and every motor starter applies reduced-inrush starting so the 100 kW sets are never stalled by starting current.

Outcome: ~50% fuel-burn reduction in low-load operation; first-pass class approval. Concept reflects engineering scope only.

Main Switchboard — Section Concept (415/240 V, 4-source)

Aluminium construction45 °C ambientManual + auto syncMode flowcharts FCT1-6

Front-of-board arrangement concept for a hybrid FCB main switchboard: generator incomer sections with metering and parallel/protection circuits, hybrid-converter incomer with its own protection, bus-tie, shore incomer with interlock, and outgoing feeder sections.

How it works: The switchboard is built as discrete vertical sections on a common busbar. Each generator section carries its incomer ACB, kW/V/A metering, and the parallel & protection relays (reverse power, over/under-voltage and frequency) that trip the set off the bus before a fault can propagate. The bus control section holds the synchroscope and manual-sync controls as class-required backup to automatic synchronising. The hybrid section repeats the same protection philosophy for the converter incomer plus the mode logic that sequences gen↔battery transitions. Operating-mode flowcharts (which sources may parallel, in what order, under which interlocks) are engineered before construction and proven during FAT — every transition the crew can request is a pre-validated path, not an improvisation.

Alarm Monitoring System (AMS) — Architecture Concept

Redundant EthernetOperator workstationsExtension alarm systemUPS-backedFleet-standardised I/O

Ship automation architecture of the class deployed across a multi-hull crew-boat programme: distributed I/O collecting machinery, bilge, tank, fire and power-system signals onto a redundant 100BASE-T network serving bridge and mess-room operator workstations, with an extension alarm system for unmanned-space watchkeeping (dead-man, group alarms, data logging, trend and replay).

How it works: Field sensors (pressure, temperature, level switches, running contacts) wire into distributed I/O cabinets placed near the machinery they monitor, which digitise each point into a channel/tag database. Two independent Ethernet networks carry the data — if network A fails, B carries on without losing a single alarm. The operator workstations display alarm pages, group summaries and trends; acknowledging an alarm anywhere acknowledges it system-wide. For unmanned machinery spaces the extension alarm system routes alarms to the duty engineer's cabin panel and enforces a dead-man routine — if the engineer doesn't reset the timer, the alarm escalates to all cabins and the bridge. Everything is logged with timestamps for class survey and incident replay.

Architecture concept of the Mega-Guard-class AMS family used on fast crew boat programmes. Generic representation — not a reproduction of any project drawing.

Autonomous Tug — Remote & Autonomous Control Architecture (Concept)

Azimuth propulsionRemote pilot stationSituational-awareness visionDedicated 240 V autonomy panelFAT-validated

Control architecture of the class used on remote/autonomous harbour tug conversions: the vessel's azimuth propulsion and machinery interfaces connect to an autonomy controller, a vision/situational-awareness suite and a satellite/4G link to an onshore pilot station — with a dedicated, UPS-backed 240 V power panel feeding every autonomy consumer.

How it works: The vessel keeps her conventional bridge controls; the autonomy system sits in parallel and a command arbitration layer decides who has the con — onboard crew, the onshore remote pilot, or the autonomous controller. The vision suite fuses cameras, radar, AIS and GNSS into one situational picture that streams to the onshore pilot station over a secured satellite/LTE link, while helm and thrust orders flow back to the azimuth drives. A watchdog supervises the link: on any loss of communications the system executes a pre-agreed fail-safe (hold position / safe-state manoeuvre) rather than continuing blind. All autonomy consumers are fed from a dedicated UPS-backed 240 V panel, segregated from ship-service distribution so an autonomy electrical fault can never take down the vessel — and vice versa. Every function was proven through staged FAT campaigns (pilot control, pilot vision, onshore station) before the system ever touched the water.
